## Why Retail Is Embracing QR Codes

**Bridging Physical and Digital**

The line between online and offline shopping has blurred beyond recognition. Customers research products online before visiting stores, browse in-store while checking online reviews, and expect seamless transitions between channels. QR codes enable this fluidity by providing instant digital access to information while customers stand in physical stores examining products.

**Solving the Information Gap**

Physical stores face an inherent challenge: limited space for product information. Packaging can only convey so much, shelf tags provide basic details, and customers often have questions that go unanswered. Sales associates can help, but they can't be everywhere at once, and customer service quality varies. QR codes solve this by making comprehensive product information instantly available without requiring staff intervention or cluttering displays.

**Reducing Operational Friction**

Traditional retail operations involve significant overhead in printing signage, updating pricing, training staff on product details, and managing inventory visibility. Every price change requires new labels. Every product launch demands new training. QR codes streamline these processes, allowing instant updates to pricing and product information without reprinting anything, and providing staff with quick access to inventory systems and product knowledge.

**Capturing Customer Data**

Unlike anonymous in-store shopping where retailers know little about browsing behavior, QR code interactions create trackable touchpoints. Research shows that 95% of businesses gather valuable first-party data through QR codes, providing insights into what products generate interest, which information customers seek, and how digital engagement correlates with purchases. This data informs everything from merchandising decisions to marketing strategies.

## Real-World Applications in Retail Stores

### 1. Product Information and Specifications

The modern shopper wants deep product knowledge before making purchasing decisions. They want to know materials, dimensions, care instructions, compatibility information, country of origin, and sustainability credentials. Fitting all this information on product packaging or shelf tags is impossible without creating cluttered, overwhelming displays that actually reduce purchase likelihood.

QR codes elegantly solve this information density problem. A small code on a product or shelf tag can link to comprehensive specifications, detailed feature lists, size charts and fit guides, material composition and care instructions, warranty information, and assembly or usage videos. This approach keeps physical displays clean and appealing while making exhaustive information available to customers who want it.

According to a GS1 US study, 79% of consumers are more willing to purchase products with a QR code that offers additional product information. The implementation works best when information architecture matches customer decision-making. Customers scanning a QR code on a coffee maker don't want to wade through marketing copy—they want to know capacity, dimensions, features, and power requirements. The landing page should present this critical information first, with additional details like sustainability certifications or company history available for those interested.

<ProTip>
Organize product information by priority: specifications first, then features, followed by care instructions and warranty details. Customers scanning QR codes are in research mode and want facts quickly.
</ProTip>

### 2. Customer Reviews and Social Proof

Purchase decisions increasingly depend on peer validation. Shoppers trust other customers more than brand messaging, with studies consistently showing that customer reviews influence buying decisions more than professional product descriptions. The challenge in physical retail is making this social proof accessible at the moment of decision.

QR codes bring the power of online reviews into physical stores. Codes on products or displays can link directly to review aggregations from multiple platforms, verified purchase reviews to ensure authenticity, photo and video reviews from real customers, and review summaries highlighting common praise and complaints. This transparency builds trust and helps customers make confident purchase decisions.

Smart retailers curate rather than simply display reviews. A QR code linking to pages with overall ratings, the most helpful positive and critical reviews highlighted, and common questions addressed creates more value than dumping customers into raw review feeds. Some stores even include responses to negative reviews, demonstrating customer service commitment and addressing common concerns proactively.

### 3. Inventory and Availability Checking

Few retail frustrations match finding a product you want only to discover your size or preferred color isn't in stock. Traditional approaches require customers to seek out staff, who may need to check backrooms or call other locations. This friction causes abandoned purchases, with many customers simply leaving to find the item elsewhere.

QR codes enable self-service inventory checking that's faster and more accurate than asking staff. Customers can scan to see all available sizes and colors at their current location, inventory at nearby store locations with directions, online availability with home delivery options, and expected restock dates for out-of-stock items. This transparency keeps customers engaged even when their first choice isn't immediately available.

The technology works particularly well for apparel, where size and color availability varies dramatically. Rather than rummaging through racks hoping to find their size, customers can scan, check availability instantly, and either grab their size or request it from the backroom or another location. This efficiency improves customer satisfaction while reducing demands on store staff.

### 4. Virtual Try-On and Visualization

Certain product categories struggle with the "will this work for me?" question that physical stores can't always answer. Furniture shoppers wonder if a sofa will fit their living room. Paint buyers can't visualize how a color will look on their walls. Glasses shoppers want to see frames on their face without trying dozens of pairs.

Augmented reality accessed through QR codes provides solutions to these visualization challenges. Furniture QR codes can trigger AR experiences that place 3D models of products in customers' actual spaces through their phone cameras, letting them see size and style fit before purchasing. Paint retailers use QR codes linking to room visualizers where customers upload photos and preview colors on their walls. Eyewear stores enable virtual try-ons where customers see frames on their face through their phone's camera.

These technologies reduce purchase anxiety and return rates simultaneously. When customers can visualize products in their intended environment, they make more confident purchase decisions and experience fewer surprises when products arrive home. The result is higher conversion rates and lower return rates—a win for both retailers and customers.

### 5. Loyalty Programs and Promotions

Traditional loyalty programs require customers to carry physical cards or remember to open apps, creating friction that reduces participation. Many potential loyalty members never enroll because the process feels cumbersome, and enrolled members forget to use their benefits because accessing them requires too many steps.

QR codes streamline loyalty program participation by making enrollment and point collection effortless. Customers can scan codes at checkout to join loyalty programs without forms or app downloads, access their current points and rewards status instantly, unlock exclusive member-only promotions, and redeem rewards without physical cards or memorized phone numbers.

The simplicity drives higher participation rates. When joining a loyalty program takes five seconds and requires minimal information, casual shoppers who would never download an app become enrolled members. Over time, these low-friction members often become loyal customers, particularly if the rewards feel valuable and easy to redeem.

### 6. Mobile Payments and Checkout

Long checkout lines represent one of retail's most persistent pain points. During busy periods, customers waiting to pay create bottlenecks that slow sales, frustrate shoppers, and require significant staffing. Even with adequate checkout stations, the payment process itself—swiping cards, entering PINs, waiting for receipts—consumes time that adds up across hundreds of daily transactions.

QR code-based mobile payments accelerate checkout substantially. Customers can scan product QR codes throughout the store, adding items to a digital cart, then complete payment directly from their phones without visiting a traditional checkout counter. Some implementations enable customers to scan a single QR code at a self-checkout station that processes their entire cart payment instantly through mobile wallets.

The benefits extend beyond speed. Mobile payments reduce the need for physical payment infrastructure, decrease cash handling and associated security concerns, create seamless paths to digital receipts, and enable easier returns by maintaining digital purchase records. Retailers also capture more customer data from mobile payments, informing future marketing and personalization efforts.

### 7. Interactive Window Displays

Retail windows serve dual purposes: attracting foot traffic during business hours and representing wasted real estate when stores are closed. Traditional window displays can't serve customers outside operating hours, meaning potentially interested shoppers walking by at night have no way to engage with products or make purchases.

QR codes transform windows into 24/7 sales channels. Codes displayed prominently in windows can link to product pages where customers can purchase items for delivery, access current promotions and sale information, browse the full catalog even when the store is closed, and save items to wishlists for later in-store visits. This extends selling hours far beyond traditional store operations.

The approach works particularly well for impulse purchases and unique items. A striking window display might capture attention at 10 PM when the store is closed, but a QR code ensures that interest can convert to a sale through online ordering. Some retailers even report higher conversion rates from window QR codes during closed hours, as browsers aren't distracted by the full store environment and can focus solely on featured items.

### 8. Product Registration and Warranties

Electronics, appliances, and other durable goods traditionally require customers to manually register products for warranties—filling out cards, visiting websites, entering serial numbers. The process is tedious enough that most customers never complete registration, leaving them without warranty protection and manufacturers without customer contact information for recalls or support.

QR codes simplify product registration dramatically. Codes on products or in packaging can link to pre-filled registration forms with product information already populated, automatic warranty activation upon submission, instant access to digital proof of purchase, and direct links to user manuals and support resources. The reduced friction increases registration completion rates substantially.

This benefits both customers and retailers. Customers ensure warranty protection and get easier access to support when needed. Retailers and manufacturers build customer databases, enabling future marketing, support outreach, and recall notifications. Some companies even use registration data to offer personalized accessory recommendations based on purchased products.

### 10. Staff Training and Product Knowledge

Retail staff face impossible product knowledge expectations. Stores carry hundreds or thousands of items, each with specific features, benefits, compatibility requirements, and common questions. New products arrive constantly. Even highly trained staff can't memorize everything, leading to situations where customers ask questions employees can't confidently answer.

QR codes provide staff with instant access to comprehensive product information. Codes on products, shelf tags, or in backroom areas can link to detailed specifications and feature breakdowns, common customer questions and recommended responses, competitive comparison information, stock levels and reorder status, and related products for upselling and cross-selling. The implementation is particularly valuable for seasonal employees or high-turnover positions where extensive training isn't feasible. New staff can provide knowledgeable service immediately by scanning products and accessing information rather than spending weeks memorizing catalogs. The approach also ensures consistency—all staff access the same information and provide uniform answers to customer questions.

### 11. Size Guides and Fit Information

Apparel and footwear face unique challenges in physical retail. Size standards vary by brand, fit preferences differ by individual, and customers often struggle to determine the right size without trying multiple options. This uncertainty drives fitting room congestion, purchase hesitation, and high return rates when customers guess incorrectly.

Comprehensive size guides accessed through QR codes reduce this friction significantly. Codes on clothing can link to detailed size charts with measurements, fit guides explaining whether items run large or small, customer reviews about sizing accuracy, style recommendations for different body types, and comparison charts for sizing across different brands in the store.

Some retailers integrate measurement tools into QR code experiences, allowing customers to input measurements and receive specific size recommendations for that item. Others include fit photos from customers of various body types, providing realistic expectations about how garments fit on different bodies. These tools increase purchase confidence and reduce returns from sizing errors.

QR codes on paint cans can link to application videos and room inspiration galleries. Craft supplies can connect to project ideas and technique tutorials. ## Implementation Tips for Retailers

### Start with High-Value Touchpoints

The breadth of QR code applications can feel overwhelming when planning retail implementation. Trying to execute everything simultaneously dilutes focus and often results in mediocre execution across all applications. A smarter approach identifies the specific customer pain points or operational inefficiencies most worth solving, then implements targeted solutions exceptionally well.

For stores with complex products requiring detailed specifications, start with product information QR codes. For apparel retailers struggling with high return rates, prioritize size guides and fit information. For stores facing checkout bottlenecks, focus on mobile payment and self-checkout solutions. This targeted approach delivers measurable results quickly while teaching lessons applicable to future implementations.

### Design for the In-Store Context

QR codes in retail exist within a specific physical and cognitive context that differs dramatically from other environments. Customers in stores are often moving, browsing multiple products, dealing with ambient noise and varying lighting, and potentially shopping with children or in groups. QR code implementations must accommodate these realities to succeed.

Placement needs particular attention. Codes should be at eye level when possible, positioned where customers naturally pause to examine products, and sized appropriately for the scanning distance. A QR code on a high shelf requires a larger size than one on a counter where customers stand directly adjacent. Lighting matters too—codes in bright window displays need higher contrast than those in interior store areas.

The destination pages must load quickly even on spotty in-store WiFi or cellular connections. Complex pages with heavy images or videos frustrate customers and cause abandonment. Keep initial load content minimal and essential, allowing deeper information to load progressively for customers who want it.

### Test Across Devices and Conditions

The diversity of smartphones customers use creates real scanning challenges that many retailers discover only after deployment. A QR code that scans perfectly on the latest iPhone might struggle on older Android devices with slower cameras. Testing must span device types, operating systems, camera qualities, and screen sizes to ensure universal accessibility.

Beyond device testing, environmental testing is crucial. Test codes in actual store lighting conditions—bright windows, dim corners, spotlit displays. Test them at the distances customers will actually scan from. Test with people wearing glasses, with shaky hands, with different levels of technical comfort. Each test reveals potential issues before customers encounter them.

### Provide Clear Value Propositions

Never assume customers understand why they should scan a QR code. The value must be immediately obvious through clear, benefit-focused labeling. "Scan for free shipping" works better than just a QR code. "See this in your room" immediately conveys the AR visualization benefit. "Check sizes available" tells customers exactly what they'll get.

The labeling should also manage expectations about what happens after scanning. If the code opens a video, say so—"Watch how it works." If it leads to a form, be clear—"Join our VIP list." Surprises after scanning generally frustrate rather than delight customers. Clear communication respects customer time and builds trust in your QR code implementations.

<Callout type="warning" title="Context Is Everything">
A QR code without context is a missed opportunity. Always include clear text explaining what customers get by scanning: "See all colors," "Read reviews," "Check stores nearby," etc. Specificity drives scans.
</Callout>

### Integrate with Existing Systems

QR codes work best when integrated into broader retail systems rather than existing as isolated implementations. A product information QR code gains significant value when connected to inventory systems, allowing customers to see real-time stock levels. Loyalty program QR codes should integrate with existing CRM and point-of-sale systems to ensure consistent customer data across channels.

This integration requires planning and often technical development, but the payoff in functionality and customer experience is substantial. Isolated QR codes might provide basic information, but integrated systems create seamless experiences that truly bridge physical and digital retail. Work with your POS provider, inventory management system, and e-commerce platform to ensure QR implementations connect to existing infrastructure.

### Monitor Performance and Iterate

Implementation doesn't end at deployment. Tracking QR code performance provides critical insights into customer behavior, preferences, and pain points. Analytics should capture scan rates, conversion rates after scanning, most and least popular code placements, peak scanning times and days, and customer actions taken after scanning.

This data drives optimization. If product information codes see high scans but low subsequent purchases, perhaps the information provided doesn't match customer needs. If window display codes drive strong conversion during evening hours, consider featuring different products optimized for browsing buyers. Let customer behavior guide refinements rather than making assumptions about what works.

Many retailers run A/B tests with QR codes, trying different placements, labels, or destinations to determine optimal approaches. Even simple tests—like "See full details" versus "Read reviews" as QR code labels—can reveal meaningful preferences that improve overall performance.

### Train Staff on QR Code Features

Staff must understand and embrace QR code implementations to maximize their effectiveness. Employees should know what each QR code does, how to help customers who struggle to scan, when to direct customers to relevant codes, and how QR codes integrate with their workflows. Without this knowledge, staff may view QR codes as threats to their roles rather than tools that enhance their effectiveness.

Training should emphasize how QR codes free staff to focus on high-value customer interactions. Instead of answering repetitive specification questions, staff can focus on style advice, problem-solving, and relationship building. Rather than checking inventory in back rooms, they can direct customers to real-time stock checking via QR codes while staying on the floor for other customers.

Some retailers incentivize staff to promote QR code usage, recognizing employees who effectively guide customers to self-service tools. This builds positive associations and ensures consistent messaging about QR code benefits across the entire team.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

Even well-intentioned QR code implementations fail when retailers neglect certain fundamentals. Understanding common pitfalls helps avoid frustrating customers and wasting implementation resources.

### Pointing to Desktop Websites

The most critical requirement for any retail QR code is a genuinely mobile-optimized destination. QR codes scanned from stores open on mobile devices, and desktop websites crammed onto phone screens create terrible experiences. Text becomes unreadable, buttons are impossible to tap accurately, and information architectures designed for large screens confuse on small displays.

Mobile optimization means building specifically for mobile use from the ground up. Navigation must be thumb-friendly with tap targets at least 44x44 pixels. Text must be readable without zooming, typically 16px minimum for body copy. Forms should minimize typing through smart defaults, dropdowns, and pre-filled fields when possible. Critical information must appear above the fold without requiring scrolling.

<Callout type="warning" title="Mobile-First Is Non-Negotiable">
If your QR code destination isn't mobile-optimized, don't launch it. A poor mobile experience is worse than no QR code at all—it frustrates customers and damages perception of your brand's technical competence.
</Callout>

### Generic, One-Size-Fits-All Content

Different products and categories require different information architectures and content priorities. A QR code on electronics should lead to specifications, compatibility information, and feature comparisons. A code on clothing should prioritize size guides, fit information, and style suggestions. A generic product page template applied across all categories serves no category well.

Effective retail QR codes provide targeted, relevant content matched to customer needs at that moment. Customers scanning a code on furniture want dimensions, material details, and delivery information. They don't want brand history or manufacturing process details (though these can appear lower on the page for interested browsers). Understanding customer intent for each product category ensures QR codes deliver value rather than just information.

### Neglecting In-Stock Customers

QR codes should enhance the in-store experience, not replace human service or redirect all customers online. Some retailers make the mistake of treating QR codes as pure online conversion tools, using them primarily to drive app downloads or push customers to e-commerce channels. This approach frustrates customers who want to purchase immediately in-store.

QR codes should serve the customer's current intent. If they're in your store, they likely want to buy in-store. Information codes should help them make purchase decisions, not distract them with online alternatives. Inventory codes should highlight in-store availability first, with nearby store and online options as backups. The goal is facilitating purchases through the customer's preferred channel, not forcing channel shifts.

### Ignoring Privacy Concerns

QR code interactions create data that many retailers are eager to collect, but aggressive data collection can backfire spectacularly. Requiring extensive personal information before providing basic product details frustrates customers and reduces scan rates. Forms asking for email, phone, birthdate, and address just to see product specifications feel invasive and exploitative.

Respect customer privacy by collecting only information essential for the immediate interaction. Product information should be accessible without any data collection. Loyalty program enrollment reasonably requires contact information, but keep forms minimal. Transparency about data usage builds trust—let customers know what you'll do with collected information and give them control over marketing communications.

Some customers remain wary of QR codes due to security concerns or privacy preferences. Always offer non-QR alternatives for accessing information. Physical signage, staff assistance, and printed materials should remain available for customers who prefer them. Choice demonstrates respect and ensures accessibility for all customers.

### Poor Print Quality and Placement

QR codes must be printed at sufficient resolution and size to scan reliably. Low-resolution printing creates pixelated codes that cameras struggle to read. Insufficient contrast—like dark gray codes on black backgrounds—makes scanning nearly impossible. Glossy finishes can create glare that interferes with camera focus.

Placement matters as much as print quality. Codes positioned too high or too low require awkward camera angles. Codes placed where customers don't naturally pause—like on baseboards or high shelves—rarely get scanned. Consider the customer journey through your store and place codes at natural decision points where customers pause to consider products.

Size requirements vary by scanning distance. Codes meant to be scanned from several feet away need to be larger than those scanned from arm's length. As a general rule, QR codes should be at least 2x2 inches (5x5 cm), with larger sizes for greater scanning distances or challenging lighting conditions.

### Forgetting to Update Content

The great advantage of QR codes over printed materials is that destination content can be updated instantly. The great failure of many implementations is neglecting to actually update content. Customers scanning codes and finding outdated pricing, discontinued products, or expired promotions quickly lose trust in QR code information.

Establish clear processes for maintaining QR code content. Who's responsible for updating product information? How quickly are prices updated when they change? How do seasonal promotions get reflected in QR code destinations? These operational questions matter as much as technical implementation.

Some retailers solve this through integration with existing systems—QR codes pull information directly from inventory and pricing databases, ensuring information stays current automatically. This approach requires more sophisticated implementation but eliminates manual update requirements and ensures accuracy.

## Getting Started with Retail QR Codes

Creating effective QR codes for your retail store is more straightforward than many owners expect. The technical barriers are minimal, and the time investment for basic implementations is modest. Here's how to approach common retail applications.

### For Product Information

The foundation of product information QR codes is organizing existing product details into mobile-friendly formats. Most retailers already have this information in inventory systems, product databases, or e-commerce platforms. The challenge is presenting it effectively on mobile devices where space is limited and customer attention spans are short.

Start by creating simple mobile pages with critical information: product name, specifications, key features, pricing, and availability. These pages can be built using accessible platforms like Shopify (if you have an e-commerce site), Google Sites for simple information pages, or dedicated product information management systems. The key is mobile optimization—test pages on various phone sizes to ensure readability and usability.

Once product pages exist, generate QR codes pointing to them. These codes can be printed on shelf talkers, product tags, or display signage. Start with your highest-value or most complex products where customer questions are most common. Monitor which products generate the most scans to understand where customers seek additional information.

<ProTip title="Start with Your Most-Asked Products">
Begin QR code implementation with products that generate the most customer questions or require the most staff explanation. These high-value applications demonstrate clear ROI and build confidence for broader rollout.
</ProTip>

### For Inventory Checking

Inventory QR codes require integration with your existing inventory management system. If you have real-time inventory visibility in your POS or warehouse management system, this data can be exposed through simple web interfaces that QR codes point to.

For retailers without sophisticated systems, start simpler. Create QR codes that link to product pages showing all available variants (sizes, colors, styles). Even without real-time inventory, showing all options helps customers understand what exists and request specific items from staff. As systems mature, you can add real-time stock levels and nearby store availability.

The most effective inventory codes are product-specific, linking directly to that item's variants and availability rather than requiring customers to search your full catalog after scanning. This specificity reduces friction and gets customers to relevant information immediately.

### For Loyalty Programs

Loyalty program QR codes work best when integrated with existing CRM and POS systems to ensure seamless point tracking and redemption. However, even simple implementations can add value. Basic approaches include using QR codes to drive customers to digital signup forms that feed into email marketing platforms, or creating unique QR codes for checkout areas that staff can scan to apply loyalty discounts.

The key is reducing friction in enrollment and participation. Every form field, required app download, or extra step reduces participation rates dramatically. The most successful programs collect minimal information initially—often just email or phone number—and gather additional details over time as customers engage with the program.

Consider tiered approaches where initial enrollment requires almost nothing, but customers can optionally provide more information to unlock additional benefits. This respects different comfort levels while still building your customer database.

### For Window Displays

Window QR codes serve customers outside operating hours, so they must link to fully functional purchasing paths. This typically means directing customers to your e-commerce site, specifically to featured products or collections highlighted in the window display.

Create dedicated landing pages for window display campaigns rather than sending all scans to your homepage. A customer scanning a code next to a specific product wants to learn about and potentially purchase that item, not navigate your entire catalog. The landing page should feature the displayed product prominently, with clear purchasing paths and relevant related items.

Include compelling calls-to-action on window signage: "Take me home tonight," "Shop this look," or "Order for delivery" make the value proposition clear. Test QR code size for window displays carefully—customers may be scanning from several feet away, requiring larger codes than typical in-store applications.

Evening and weekend scans (when the store was closed) generated 30% of online sales, effectively extending selling hours without additional staffing.
</Example>

## The Future of QR Codes in Retail

QR code technology in retail continues evolving, with emerging applications that promise to further transform how customers shop and how stores operate. Understanding these trends helps retailers plan implementations that remain relevant as technology advances.

**Augmented Reality Integration**

Research shows that 92% of businesses believe AR-powered QR codes will see widespread adoption. Current AR implementations via QR codes are just the beginning. Future applications will include virtual fitting rooms where customers scan products and see themselves wearing items without physically trying them on, home furnishing visualization showing multiple product combinations in customer spaces simultaneously, and interactive product demonstrations where customers manipulate 3D models to understand features and functionality.

As AR technology matures and smartphone cameras improve, these experiences will become more realistic and widely adopted. Retailers investing in QR code infrastructure now position themselves to easily integrate AR features as they become more accessible and customer expectations evolve.

**Personalization and AI Integration**

Research indicates that 39% of consumers now expect personalized experiences when scanning QR codes. Future QR code experiences will likely remember customer preferences and purchase history, providing personalized recommendations when they scan products. Artificial intelligence will enable QR codes to offer tailored suggestions based on browsing behavior, previous purchases, and stated preferences.

Imagine scanning a product and immediately seeing recommendations based on your specific style, budget, and past purchases rather than generic suggestions shown to all customers. This level of personalization creates more valuable experiences and drives higher conversion rates by surfacing truly relevant alternatives and complementary products.

**Voice-Enabled Shopping**

Voice assistants and voice search continue improving, suggesting future QR implementations may integrate voice capabilities. Customers could scan a code and speak questions or requests: "Is this available in blue?" or "Show me similar items under $50." Voice interactions feel natural and reduce the typing friction that mobile shopping sometimes creates.

Early experiments with voice-enabled QR codes show promise, particularly for accessibility. Customers with visual impairments or motor difficulties that make typing challenging benefit significantly from voice alternatives that QR codes can enable.

**Blockchain for Supply Chain Transparency**

Blockchain technology offers unprecedented supply chain transparency, and QR codes provide the interface for customers to access this information. Future implementations may allow customers to scan products and trace complete journeys from raw materials through manufacturing, shipping, and retail arrival.

This transparency particularly appeals to customers prioritizing sustainability, ethical manufacturing, and product authenticity. Luxury goods retailers are already exploring blockchain-verified authenticity certificates accessible via QR codes, helping combat counterfeiting while providing customers with verifiable proof of authenticity.

**Seamless Omnichannel Experiences**

The future of retail is genuinely channel-agnostic, with customers moving fluidly between online, mobile, and in-store experiences.
